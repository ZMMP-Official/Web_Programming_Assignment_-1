Personal Development

Here is the Personal Developmet Segment of the Assignment

I Have read chapters 4 – 7 of "Twelve Pillars" by Jim Rohn and Chris Widener [pp. 43 – 80]. This is my personal reflection on the chapters.

First Pillar: Achieve Your Goals (pages 43-52)

In this chapter, Michael meets Charlie at Mack's Diner, where they discuss Michael's progress with his relationships and then delve into the crucial topic of goal-setting.
Charlie emphasizes that Michael's lack of career progression might be directly linked to not having clear goals.
The core message from Mr. Davis is simple yet profound: "Write it down." Writing down goals, especially a list of one hundred things you want to achieve before you die, brings them into reality and makes your mind "cognitively aware" of them.
Charlie also shares Mr. Davis's powerful wisdom: "The major reason for setting a goal is for what it makes of you to accomplish it."
The personal growth and transformation required to achieve a goal are often more valuable than the goal itself.
The chapter introduces the concept of setting long-range (10-year), mid-range (5-year), and short-range (1-2 year) goals, along with "micro-goals" (monthly), all designed to lead to the ultimate objective.
Mr. Davis famously states, "You cannot change your destination overnight, but you can change your direction overnight."

Personal Insight

After I read this chapeter I can relate this to my own change in life during past struggles.
Feeling the imense weight of a goal left me hopeless, but having an objectives list or just writing it down.
Gave me drive, the goal wasn't an insurmountable mountain to climb anymore.

Second Pillar: The Proper Use of Time (pages 53-56)

Michael expresses his struggle with finding time for goal-setting amid his busy life. Charlie responds with a powerful quote from Mr. Davis: "We must all suffer from one of two pains: The pain of discipline or the pain of regret. The difference is discipline weighs ounces while regret weighs tons."
This highlights the long-term cost of inaction versus the short-term effort of discipline.
Charlie emphasizes that "days are expensive" and must be spent wisely, focusing on the "one best opportunity" that aligns with overall goals, rather than just reacting to the "urgent." Mr. Davis warns that if you don't design your own life plan, you'll fall into someone else's, who likely hasn't planned "much" for you.
The chapter concludes with the idea that "Either you run the day or the day runs you. Either you control your time or it controls you. Take your pick."

Personal Insight

This whole chapter describes both the misery and the salvation to it in my life.
I've had always had a bad sense of time when it comes to telling the estimated amout of time for an activity.
Setting my own schedule has saved me from the nightmare of procrastination.

Third Pillar: Surround Yourself With the Best People (pages 57-70)

Michael shares his progress with Charlie, including his written goals. Charlie then introduces the Pillar of "Surround yourself with the best."
This doesn't mean material possessions, but rather the "best people"—winners and successful individuals whose values and skills you admire.
Mr. Davis's critical questions are: "Who am I around? What effect are they having on me? What have they got me reading? What have they got me saying? Where do they have me going? What do they have me thinking? And most importantly, what do they have me becoming? Then ask yourself the big question: Is that okay?"
Charlie explains that every association has a positive, neutral, or negative effect, leading to three types: disassociation, limited association, and expanded association.
He uses the analogy of maintaining a pool's pH balance to illustrate how relationships need careful management to avoid negative influences. He challenges Michael to consider if his long-time friends are "friends of convenience" rather than those who challenge him to grow.
Mr. Davis's advice for attracting successful people is: "To attract attractive people you must be attractive. To attract powerful people you must be powerful. To attract committed people you must be committed.
Instead of going to work on them, go to work on yourself.
If you become, you can attract."
Surrounding yourself with successful people leads to new knowledge, a vast network, and most importantly, a positive attitude, which Mr. Davis believes is "ninety-nine percent of life."

Personal Insight

This chapter makes me feel greatful for my group of friends.

Fourth Pillar: Be a Life-Long Learner (pages 71-80)

Charlie and Michael discuss the importance of continuous learning. Charlie reveals Mr. Davis's next Pillar: "A successful person is always a life-long learner."
A key distinction is made between formal education and self-education: "Formal education will make you a living. Self-education will make you a fortune."
Michael initially dismisses reading, but Charlie passionately argues for its value, quoting Mr. Davis: "It isn't what the book costs. It's what it will cost you if you don't read it."
Books open new worlds, ideas, and are seeds for achievement.Mr. Davis's wisdom continues: "If someone is going down the wrong path, he doesn't need motivation to speed him up.He needs education to turn him around. Learning is the beginning of wealth. Learning is the beginning of health. Learning is the beginning of spirituality. Searching and learning is where the miracle process all begins."
The three mainstays of learning are identified as reading books, learning from successful people by observation, and constantly reflecting on personal experiences.
The chapter ends with Charlie giving Michael a copy of "The Magic of Thinking Big," insisting he read it before their next meeting.

Personal Insight

A golden rule of my life that I follow and was taught early by the most important in my life.
